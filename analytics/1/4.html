How It Works

RSI Strategy

The RSI (Relative Strength Index) is a momentum oscillator that measures the speed and magnitude of price changes on a scale from 0 to 100. It compares the average gain to the average loss over 14 periods. When RSI rises above 70, the asset is considered overbought — the price has risen too fast and a pullback or consolidation is likely. When RSI drops below 30, the asset is oversold — selling pressure is exhausted and a bounce is expected. The classic strategy is to sell (or take profits) when RSI enters overbought territory and buy when it enters oversold territory. More advanced traders look for divergences: if price makes a new high but RSI makes a lower high, it signals weakening momentum and a potential reversal.

Backtest Methodology

Starting with a $10,000 portfolio, the RSI strategy buys when RSI crosses back above 30 (exit oversold) and sells when it crosses back below 70 (exit overbought). The equity curve is compared against a simple Buy & Hold strategy. RSI tends to generate fewer trades than MACD — it only triggers at extremes, making it more selective but potentially missing trending moves.
