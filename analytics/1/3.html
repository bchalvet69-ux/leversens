How It Works

MACD Strategy

The MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence) measures the relationship between two exponential moving averages to identify trend changes and momentum. The MACD line (EMA 12 minus EMA 26) oscillates around zero — when it's positive, the short-term trend is faster than the long-term, signaling bullish momentum. The Signal line (EMA 9 of MACD) acts as a trigger: a MACD cross above the signal is a buy signal, and a cross below is a sell signal. The histogram (bars) shows the gap between MACD and signal — growing bars mean accelerating momentum, shrinking bars mean momentum is fading. A powerful signal is divergence: when price makes a new high but MACD doesn't, it warns that the uptrend is losing steam and a reversal may follow.

Backtest Methodology

Starting with a $10,000 portfolio, the MACD strategy goes long when the MACD line crosses above the Signal line (bullish crossover) and sells the full position when it crosses below (bearish crossover). The equity curve is compared against a simple Buy & Hold strategy that invests the same $10,000 at the start and holds throughout. This highlights whether the timing signals add value over passive investing.
