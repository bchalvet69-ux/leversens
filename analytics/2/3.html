What is ARIMA?

ARIMA (AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average) forecasts prices based on past patterns. AR uses past values, I applies differencing for stationarity, and MA captures forecast error persistence. It produces a 30-day forecast with confidence intervals that widen over time as uncertainty grows.

Limitations

ARIMA assumes linear patterns and struggles with regime changes and extreme volatility. The model validation alert above tells you whether the model's assumptions hold for the selected asset. If residuals show autocorrelation (Ljung-Box test fails), the forecast should be treated with caution — it may not capture the asset's true dynamics.

Residual Analysis

Good residuals should look like random noise with no visible patterns. If the residuals show trends or clustering, the model missed some structure and forecasts may be unreliable. Scattered residuals centered around zero confirm the ARIMA model captured the data's temporal patterns correctly.

Model Validation

The ACF (Autocorrelation Function) checks if residuals are truly random. All bars should stay within the red confidence bands. Bars extending beyond indicate the model needs refinement. Green bars are within bounds (good), red bars exceed them (problematic).
